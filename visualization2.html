State Trends – Total Penalties by Jurisdiction (2008–2024)

This page allows users to examine how road safety enforcement differs across Australian jurisdictions from 2008 to 2024. The visualisations display both the Total Penalty Trends and the Penalty Distribution, offering insight into whether variations are due to stricter policing or differences in population sizes.
Use the filters above to select specific violation types, detection methods, and age groups. The charts update dynamically based on your selections, and hovering reveals detailed values such as fines, charges, and arrests.

Trend of Total Penalties (All Jurisdictions)

This chart highlights yearly penalty trends for all Australian states and territories.

Each jurisdiction is represented with a unique colour, which is easy to identify the pattern between lines.

The trend clearly shows that Victoria records the highest penalty peak in 2018, while other states display more gradual changes throughout the 16-year period.

This histogram illustrates the statistical distribution of penalty counts based on the chosen filter settings.

By grouping penalty values into bins, the chart highlights important characteristics of the dataset, such as skewness, clustering, or the presence of extreme values.

This distribution-oriented perspective complements the multi-line chart by showing how penalty magnitudes vary, rather than how they change over time.

Penalty Distribution

Combined, these visualisations support meaningful comparisons of road safety enforcement activity across Australia. By providing both total and distribution-based insights, this page encourages fair and evidence-based evaluation of each state's road enforcement policies.

The dataset includes several limitations that may affect interpretation. Manual enforcement speeding data is incomplete prior to 2016. Unlicensed driving and age group categories are mainly recorded from 2023–2024. Variations in population, data coverage, and the number of detection cameras across states influence penalty counts. Therefore, figures should be viewed as indicative rather than directly comparable across jurisdictions.

Data sourced from the Bureau of Infrastructure and Transport Research Economics (BITRE)
