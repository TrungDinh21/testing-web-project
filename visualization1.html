Australia’s Road Safety: A National Overview of Traffic Penalties (2008–2024)

This page provides a nationwide view of traffic law enforcement across Australia from 2008 to 2024. By combining a heat map and a time-series line chart, users can explore how penalties for offences such as speeding, unlicensed driving, mobile phone use, and seatbelt violations vary across states and evolve over time. Use the filters to uncover trends or compare jurisdictions, from the busiest roads of Victoria to the quieter territories in the north.

Heat Map Section – Regional Distribution

The heat map reveals striking contrasts between states.
New South Wales (NSW) and Victoria (VIC) consistently record the highest number of traffic penalties, reflecting their larger populations and higher vehicle activity.
In contrast, smaller jurisdictions like the Northern Territory (NT) and the Australian Capital Territory (ACT) show far fewer offences, often fewer than 50,000 annually.

This spatial view highlights how enforcement intensity and driver behaviour differ nationwide, an essential context before exploring time-based trends.

Line Chart Section – Trends Over Time

From 2008 to 2024, overall penalties across Australia have fluctuated rather than followed a single direction.
- New South Wales (NSW) shows a sharp rise from around 600,000 to over one million by 2021.
- Queensland (QLD) shows two major surges and stabilises more than 900,000.
- Western Australia (WA) peaks around 2022 and has fallen in the following years.
- Victoria (VIC) trends downward after 2018.
- Tasmania (TAS) declines notably between 2012 and 2019.
- ACT remains consistently low with minor fluctuations.

Overall, Australia’s traffic enforcement shows waves of rise and fall rather than a single trend. Yet, each state tells a different story, from New South Wales' dramatic spikes to Tasmania’s steady decline.
Explore the State Trend page to uncover what drives these differences.

The dataset includes several limitations that may affect interpretation. Manual enforcement speeding data is incomplete prior to 2016. Unlicensed driving and age group categories are mainly recorded from 2023–2024. Variations in population, data coverage, and the number of detection cameras across states influence penalty counts. Therefore, figures should be viewed as indicative rather than directly comparable across jurisdictions.

Data sourced from the Bureau of Infrastructure and Transport Research Economics (BITRE)
